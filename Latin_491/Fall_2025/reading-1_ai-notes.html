Sentences 1

Translate these sentences into English and be ready to explain your work.

1
Malum mihi videtur esse mors.
  • Malum: best taken as a substantive here, = "an evil"
  • videtur: video = "see"; in the passive it often = "seem"
2
Is -ne, qui mortui sunt, an is, quibus moriendum est?
  • Is = iīs: when used adverbially, DAT(X) can indicate for whom the action or situation holds true.
  • quibus: when dependent on a gerundive, DAT(X) indicates the agent for whom the obligation holds: = "gerundive(G) must be carried out by (DAT(X).
  • moriendum est: when a transitive verb is used in the gerundive, the normal rules of passivization apply, and what would be the direct object of the active appears as the SBJ of the passive. When the verb is intransitive, as here, the verb is used "impersonally." In such cases, the verb becomes, in a way, its own SBJ. So moriendum est means something like "a death must be died" or "an act of dying must be carried out." This not satisfactory English. It is often preferable to make the agent of a gerundive the SBJ of an active verb.
3
Utrisque.
  • Utrisque: when used adverbially, DAT(X) can indicate for whom the action or situation holds true. = "for both."
4
Est miserum igitur, quoniam malum.
  • miserum: This adjective indicates the subjective psychological state that follows from the presence of malum, an objective "evil."
  • Note that ellipsis is common in conversation (and its literary representation). The verb of the quoniam clause is not expressed but is implicit by context.
5
Certe.
  • Another ellipsis.
6
Ergo et i, quibus evenit iam ut morerentur, et i, quibus eventurum est, miseri.
  • i = iī; the adjective/pronoun is, ea, id has many morphological variants.
  • evenit: = "happened"
  • ut morerentur: evenit often occurs with an ut + subjunctive clause as SBJ; translate with English that-clause.
  • eventurum est: → prospective present active → s-form(be) + "going to" + lemma-form(verb X)
  • miseri: another ellipsis
7
Mihi ita videtur.
  • ita: = "thus, so" in the sense of "like that."
  • videtur: video = "see"; in the passive it often = "seem"
8
Nemo ergo non miser.
  • Ellpsis of verb
9
Prorsus nemo.
  • Ellpsis of verb
10
Et quidem, si tibi constare vis, omnes, quicumque nati sunt eruntve, non solum miseri, sed etiam semper miseri.
  • quidem: a discourse particle/sentence adverbial. quidem is emphatic. Here the emphasis indicates that sentence 10 is a stronger/clearer version of the point made in sentence 8.
  • constare: sentence frame = constare(SBJ(X), DAT(Y)) → SBJ(X) "agrees/is consistent with Y."
  • quicumque; = "whoever"
  • nati sunt eruntve: = nati sunt aut nati erunt
  • miseri ... miseri: ellipses of verbs
11
nam si solos eos diceres miseros quibus moriendum esset, neminem tu quidem eorum qui viverent exciperes — moriendum est enim omnibus —, esset tamen miseriae finis in morte.
  • diceres: in this context imperfect subjuctive → progressive present unreal (unreal = contrary to fact) → "were" + ing-form(verb X); "were" is the past subjunctive of English "be." Relevant verb frame of dicere → dicere(SBJ(X), OBJ(ACC1(Y)), OCOMP(ACC2(Z)) → SBJ(X) "calls/names" Y Z"
  • quibus: when dependent on a gerundive, DAT(X) indicates the agent for whom the obligation holds: = "gerundive(G) must be carried out by (DAT(X).
  • moriendum esset: subjuctive verbs are regularly found in clauses subordinate to indirect statements. Such cases need no special translation in English.
  • quidem: emphasizes the preceding word.
  • exciperes: = "exclude." imperfect subjuctive → progressive present unreal → "would be" + ing-form(verb X).
  • omnibus: when dependent on a gerundive, DAT(X) indicates the agent for whom the obligation holds: = "gerundive(G) must be carried out by (DAT(X)
  • esset: imperfect subjuctive → progressive present unreal → "would be"; note that "be" is a stative verb and English generally avoids the use of the ing-form (e.g., "woud be being") except in special circumstances.
12
quoniam autem etiam mortui miseri sunt, in miseriam nascimur sempiternam.
  • etiam: a focus particle, here puts emphasis on the next word: = "even the dead ..."
13
necesse est enim miseros esse eos qui centum milibus annorum ante occiderunt, vel potius omnis, quicumque nati sunt.
  • necesse est: English often uses an empty placeholder when the SBJ of this construction is a clause: = "it is necessary that ..."
  • centum milibus: ABL(X) without a prep. may indicate distance or "degree of difference." Here = "100 thousands of years earlier/earlier by 100 thousands of years."
  • quicumque: = "whoever"
14
Ita prorsus existimo.
  • Ita: = "so," as in "I think so."
15
Dic quaeso: num te illa terrent, triceps apud inferos Cerberus, Cocyti fremitus, travectio Acherontis, mento summam aquam attingens enectus siti Tantalus?
  • quaeso: = "I beg/pray" ≈ "please"
  • num: untranslatable, indicates the clause is a question
  • triceps, MASC SING NOM adj. = :three headed"
  • Cocyti: Cocytus, -i, mas. Proper PLace Name, a river in the underworld
  • Acherontis: Acheron, Acherontis, m. PPLN, a river in the underworld
  • mento: = "chin"; ABL(X) without prep. = with/by means of X
  • siti: ABL(X) without prep. = with/by means of X
  • Tantalus: Tantalus, -i, m. PrN
16
tum illud, quod Sisyphus versat saxum sudans nitendo ne -que proficit hilum?
  • quod: = the conjunction "that." A quod/"that" clause often occurs as the subject of a verb. Here it is the subject of the ellipsed main verb of the sentence.
  • illud, quod: appossition of a clause and a neuter demonstrative pronoun is common in Latin.
  • Sisyphus: Sisyphus, -i, m. PrN
  • nitendo: a gerund, = "working/striving/making an effort"; ABL(X) without prep. = with/by means of X
  • hilum: = "a trifle"; most often used with a negative = "not [even] a trifle."
17
fortasse etiam inexorabiles iudices, Minos et Rhadamanthus?
  • iudices: the verb of this clause has been ellipsed.
  • Minos: PrN, MASC SING NOM
  • Rhadamanthus, PrN, Rhadamanthus, Rhadamanthi, m.
18
apud quos ne -c te L. Crassus defendet ne -c M. Antonius ne -c, quoniam apud Graecos iudices res agetur, poteris adhibere Demosthenen;
  • apud: prep + ACC(X) is often used of judges hearing a case; apud quos = "before whom."
  • quos: Latin does not hesitate to begin a sentence with a relative word connecting the new sentence to the previous context. English avoids this use.
  • L. Crassus: PrN, a famous orator
  • M. Antonius: PrN, a famous orator
  • res agetur: in a legal setting, the phrase agere rem = "to transact/handle a case [at law]"
  • adhibere: = "bring in"
  • Demosthenen: a famous Greek orator
19
tibi ipsi pro te erit maxima corona causa dicenda.
  • tibi: dative of agent with the passive periphrastic; it indicates the person who has the obligation to act: DAT(X) = "by X"
  • ipsi: an intensive adjective, here modifying tibi.
  • pro: prep. + ABL(X) = "for/on behalf of X"
  • te: reflexive = yourself
  • corona: In a legal context, a corona ("crown" or "garland") refers to the circle of people, such as the jurors, judges, or spectators, at a trial. ABL(X) without prep. = "in X"; ABL(X) without prep. = "with X." Either may be the sense intended by Cicero.
  • causa: = "case" in the sense of a legal case
  • dicenda: passive periphrastic gerundive. The action (dicenda) is an obligation for the person indicated by the dative of agent (tibi).
20
haec fortasse metuis et idcirco mortem censes esse sempiternum malum.
  • haec: refers to the things just mentioned in sentences 15-17.
  • idcirco: = "for this reason" or "therefore."
  • mortem: the subject of the infinitive **esse**.
  • censes: verb of thinking, followed by an indirect statement.
21
Adeone me delirare censes, ut ista esse credam?
  • adeo: adverb meaning "to such a degree." It may be thought of as a placeholder or "empty variable" that indicates some degree or extent. Experience with this word leads the reader/listener to expect that the content of the "variable" will be filled in in short order by a "result" clause with **ut** + subjunctive.
  • -ne: indicates a yes/no question.
  • delirare: infinitive, part of an indirect statement after **censes**.
  • ista: = "those things," refers to the myths of the underworld.
  • ut ... credam: a result clause, expressing the outcome of the action in the main clause.
22
An tu haec non credis?
  • an: indicates an alternative question, usually after a previous question. The implied choice is "yes" or "no."
  • haec: refers to the same things as **ista** in the previous sentence.
23
Minime vero.
  • minime vero: "least of all, in fact." A strong negative response.
  • Ellipsis of verb.
24
Male hercule narras.
  • male: adverb, = "badly."
  • hercule: a mild interjection, from Hercules. = "by Hercules!"
  • narras: from narro, -are, to tell or relate.
25
Cur quaeso?
  • cur: interrogative adverb, = "why?"
  • quaeso: = "I beg/pray" ≈ "please."
26
Quia disertus esse possem, si contra ista dicerem.
  • quia: a conjunction introducing a causal clause.
  • disertus: an adjective meaning "eloquent."
  • possem: imperfect subjunctive, expressing unreality or contrary-to-fact condition.
  • si ... dicerem: a present unreal condition. "If I were speaking..."
  • ista: = "those things," referring to the fables of the underworld.
27
Quis enim non in eius modi causa?
  • quis: interrogative pronoun, "who?"
  • enim: a particle meaning "for, indeed," often used to explain a previous statement.
  • in eius modi: = "in a case of this sort."
  • Ellipsis of verb, likely **possem esse disertus**.
28
aut quid negotii est haec poëtarum et pictorum portenta convincere?
  • aut: a conjunction meaning "or."
  • quid negotii: a partitive genitive, literally "what of business?" = "what difficulty?"
  • convincere: infinitive, subject of **est**.
  • haec...portenta: the direct object of **convincere**.
  • poëtarum et pictorum: genitive case, = "of poets and painters."
29
Atqui pleni libri sunt contra ista ipsa disserentium philosophorum.
  • atqui: a strong adversative particle, "and yet," "but still."
  • pleni: adjective, "full," modifying **libri**.
  • contra: preposition with accusative, "against."
  • ista ipsa: "those very things," referring to the fables.
  • disserentium: a present participle, genitive plural, modifying **philosophorum**. = "speaking/arguing."
  • philosophorum: a noun in the genitive case, = "of philosophers."
30
Inepte sane.
  • inepte: adverb, "foolishly."
  • sane: an adverb emphasizing the preceding word, "indeed," "of course."
  • Ellipsis of a phrase like "you speak."
31
quis enim est tam excors, quem ista moveant?
  • quis: interrogative pronoun, "who?"
  • enim: "for, indeed," provides an explanation.
  • tam: "so," introducing a relative clause of characteristic.
  • excors: an adjective meaning "senseless," or "mad."
  • quem ... moveant: a relative clause of characteristic, describing the kind of person who is "tam excors." The subjunctive **moveant** is characteristic of this construction.
32
Si ergo apud inferos miseri non sunt, ne sunt quidem apud inferos ulli.
  • si: a conjunction introducing a conditional clause.
  • ergo: "therefore," "so."
  • apud inferos: "among the dead."
  • ne...quidem: a correlative phrase meaning "not even." It emphasizes the word it encloses, here **sunt**.
  • ulli: a pronoun, "any." With the negative **ne...quidem**, it means "none."
33
Ita prorsus existimo.
  • Ita: adverb, "so," "thus."
  • prorsus: adverb, "absolutely," "completely."
  • existimo: first person singular present indicative of **existimare**, "to think," "to judge."
34
Ubi sunt ergo i, quos miseros dicis, aut quem locum incolunt?
  • ubi: interrogative adverb, "where?"
  • ergo: "therefore."
  • i: = **ii**, nominative plural of the pronoun **is, ea, id**.
  • quos miseros dicis: a relative clause, "whom you call wretched." **quos** is the accusative object of **dicis**.
  • quem: interrogative adjective, "what," modifying **locum**.
  • incolunt: from **incolo**, -ere, to inhabit.
35
si enim sunt, nusquam esse non possunt.
  • si enim: "for if."
  • sunt: "they exist."
  • nusquam: adverb, "nowhere."
  • non possunt: "they cannot." The double negative in Latin, **non** + **nusquam**, reinforces the negation, "they cannot be nowhere" = "they must be somewhere."
36
Ego vero nusquam esse illos puto.
  • ego: an emphatic subject pronoun, "I."
  • vero: a strong particle, "in fact," "indeed."
  • nusquam esse illos: part of an indirect statement with **puto**. **illos** is the subject of the infinitive **esse**.
  • puto: "I think."
37
Igitur ne esse quidem?
  • igitur: "therefore."
  • ne...quidem: "not even." The particle emphasizes the word in between, here **esse**. The phrase means "not even to exist?"
  • Ellipsis of a main verb like **dicas** ("do you say?").
38
Prorsus isto modo, et tamen miseros ob id ipsum quidem, quia nulli sint.
  • prorsus isto modo: "absolutely in that way."
  • tamen: "nevertheless."
  • ob id ipsum: "on account of that very thing." **id** refers to the fact that they do not exist.
  • quidem: emphatic particle.
  • quia nulli sint: a causal clause. **nulli sint** is in the subjunctive mood because it expresses a reason from the speaker's viewpoint.
39
Iam mallem Cerberum metueres quam ista tam inconsiderate diceres.
  • iam: "now," or "at this point."
  • mallem: imperfect subjunctive of **malo**, "I would prefer." Used in a present unreal conditional.
  • metueres: imperfect subjunctive, part of the **quam** clause, "than you would fear."
  • quam: a conjunction used to introduce the second part of a comparison.
  • ista: "those things," referring to the person's statements about the dead being wretched.
  • inconsiderate: adverb, "thoughtlessly."
  • diceres: imperfect subjunctive.
40
Quid tandem?
  • quid: interrogative pronoun, "what?"
  • tandem: an interrogative particle, "pray," "then."
  • This phrase is used to express indignation or surprise.
41
Quem esse negas, eundem esse dicis.
  • quem ... negas: "whom you deny to exist." **quem** is the subject of the infinitive **esse**.
  • eundem: accusative, subject of **esse**, from **isdem**. "the same one."
  • esse: infinitive, subject of the verb **dicis**.
  • dicis: "you say."
  • The phrase means "you say that the same person exists whom you deny to exist."
42
ubi est acumen tuum?
  • ubi: interrogative adverb, "where?"
  • acumen: a noun, "sharpness," "acuity."
  • tuum: an adjective, "your."
43
cum enim miserum esse dicis, tum eum qui non sit dicis esse.
  • cum: "when."
  • enim: "for."
  • miserum esse dicis: indirect statement.
  • tum: "then."
  • eum qui non sit: a relative clause, "the one who does not exist." **qui** is the subject of the subjunctive **sit**.
  • dicis esse: indirect statement. The full phrase is "you say that he who does not exist does exist."
44
Non sum ita hebes, ut istud dicam.
  • ita: adverb, "so," or "to such a degree."
  • hebes: an adjective, "dull," "stupid."
  • ut istud dicam: a result clause. **dicam** is in the subjunctive mood.
  • istud: "that thing of yours," referring to the idea that he is contradicting himself.
45
Quid dicis igitur?
  • quid: interrogative pronoun, "what?"
  • dicus igitur: "what, then, do you say?"
46
Miserum esse verbi causa M. Crassum, qui illas fortunas morte dimiserit, miserum Cn. Pompeium, qui tanta gloria sit orbatus, omnis denique miseros, qui hac luce careant.
  • miserum esse: part of an indirect statement, with an implied main verb like **dico**.
  • verbi causa: "for the sake of the word," or "for example."
  • M. Crassum: accusative, subject of the infinitive **esse**.
  • qui ... dimiserit: a relative clause in the subjunctive. The subjunctive expresses cause or reason, "since he lost."
  • fortunas: "fortunes," or "wealth."
  • Cn. Pompeium: accusative, subject of an implied **esse**.
  • qui ... sit orbatus: a relative clause in the subjunctive, expressing cause. "since he was deprived."
  • tanta gloria: ablative of separation, with **orbatus**. "deprived of such great glory."
  • omnis...miseros: accusative, another subject of an implied **esse**.
  • qui hac luce careant: relative clause in the subjunctive, expressing cause. "since they lack this light."
47
Revolveris eodem.
  • revolveris: from **revolvo**, -ere, "to roll back." Here, "you return."
  • eodem: adverb, "to the same place."
48
sint enim oportet, si miseri sunt;
  • sint: subjunctive, from **sum, esse**.
  • oportet: an impersonal verb, "it is right," "it is necessary." The phrase means "for it is necessary that they exist."
  • si miseri sunt: a conditional clause.
49
tu autem modo negabas eos esse, qui mortui essent.
  • tu: emphatic subject pronoun, "you."
  • autem: "but," "however."
  • modo: adverb, "just now," "lately."
  • negabas: "you were denying."
  • eos esse, qui mortui essent: an indirect statement with a relative clause. **eos** is the subject of **esse**. **qui** is the subject of **essent**. The subjunctive **essent** is a result of it being in a subordinate clause within an indirect statement.
50
Si igitur non sunt, nihil possunt esse;
  • si igitur: "if, therefore."
  • non sunt: "they do not exist."
  • nihil: an adverbial accusative, "in no way," or "nothing."
  • possunt esse: "they are able to be." The phrase means "they can be nothing," or "they cannot exist."
51
ita ne miseri quidem sunt.
  • ita: "so," "in this way."
  • ne miseri quidem: "not even miserable." The negative particle **ne** and the particle **quidem** emphasize the word between them.
52
Non dico fortasse etiam, quod sentio;
  • non dico: "I do not say."
  • fortasse: "perhaps."
  • etiam: "even."
  • quod sentio: "that which I feel/think." The verb is in the indicative, indicating the speaker's personal feeling.
53
nam istuc ipsum, non esse, cum fueris, miserrimum puto.
  • nam: "for."
  • istuc ipsum: accusative, the subject of the infinitive **puto**. "that very thing."
  • non esse: infinitive phrase, "not to exist."
  • cum fueris: a **cum** clause, "when you have existed." The verb is in the perfect subjunctive, indicating a past condition.
  • miserrimum: superlative adjective, "most miserable."
  • puto: "I think," followed by an indirect statement.
54
Quid?
  • quid: an elliptical interrogative, "what about...?"
55
miserius quam omnino numquam fuisse?
  • miserius: comparative adjective, "more miserable."
  • quam: a conjunction, "than."
  • omnino: "entirely," "at all."
  • numquam: "never."
  • fuisse: perfect infinitive of **sum**, "to have been." The phrase means "more miserable than to have never existed at all?"
56
ita, qui nondum nati sunt, miseri iam sunt, quia non sunt, et nos, si post mortem miseri futuri sumus, miseri fuimus ante quam nati.
  • ita: "so," "in this way."
  • qui nondum nati sunt: a relative clause, "who have not yet been born."
  • iam: "already."
  • quia non sunt: a causal clause, "because they do not exist."
  • post mortem: "after death."
  • futuri sumus: future active periphrastic, "we will be."
  • fuimus: perfect indicative of **sum**, "we have been."
  • ante quam: a conjunction, "before."
  • nati: perfect passive participle of **nascor**, "born."
57
ego autem non commemini, ante quam sum natus, me miserum;
  • ego autem: "but I."
  • non commemini: "I do not remember." The verb is from **commemoror**, a deponent verb.
  • ante quam: "before."
  • sum natus: "I was born."
  • me miserum: accusative + adjective, part of an indirect statement, "that I was miserable."
58
tu si meliore memoria es, velim scire, ecquid de te recordere.
  • tu: emphatic pronoun, "you."
  • si meliore memoria es: a conditional clause, "if you are of a better memory." **memoria** is an ablative of quality.
  • velim: a potential subjunctive, "I would like."
  • scire: infinitive, "to know."
  • ecquid: an interrogative particle, "whether anything."
  • de te: "about yourself."
  • recordere: a deponent verb in the subjunctive, part of an indirect question. "whether you recall anything."
